6.9 8.6.3 Bayes Factors for Model Comparison

To compare two models \(M_1\) and \(M_2\), we consider their posterior odds:

\[ \frac{p(M_1 | D)}{p(M_2 | D)} = \underbrace{\frac{p(M_1)}{p(M_2)}}_{\text{prior odds}} \times \underbrace{\frac{p(D | M_1)}{p(D | M_2)}}_{\text{Bayes factor}} \]

  • The Bayes factor \(\frac{p(D | M_1)}{p(D | M_2)}\) measures relative model support from the data.
  • With uniform priors, model selection depends only on the Bayes factor.

If the Bayes factor > 1, \(M_1\) is preferred; otherwise, \(M_2\) is chosen.

6.9.1 Remark: Jeffreys-Lindley Paradox

This paradox notes that the Bayes factor tends to favor simpler models when complex models have diffuse priors (i.e., broad, non-informative priors).
