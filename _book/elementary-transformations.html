2.4 2.3.2 Elementary Transformations

Elementary transformations are operations that simplify a system of linear equations without changing its solution set.
These are:

  1. Row exchange: Swap two equations (rows).
  2. Row scaling: Multiply an equation (row) by a nonzero scalar \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}\).
  3. Row addition: Add a multiple of one equation (row) to another.

These operations are key to transforming a system into simpler forms, such as row-echelon form (REF) or reduced row-echelon form (RREF).

2.4.1 Example: Transforming to Row-Echelon Form

Given a system of equations (Eq. 2.44), we write it in augmented matrix form: \[ [A | b] = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 4 & -2 & -1 & 4 & -3 \\ 4 & -8 & 3 & -3 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -2 & 0 & -3 & 4 & a \end{bmatrix} \]

By applying elementary row operations, we obtain a simpler (row-echelon) form: \[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 & 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -1 & 3 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & a + 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

The resulting equations are: \[ \begin{aligned} x_1 - 2x_2 + x_3 - x_4 + x_5 &= 0 \\ x_3 - x_4 + 3x_5 &= -2 \\ x_4 - 2x_5 &= 1 \\ 0 &= a + 1 \end{aligned} \]

A particular solution exists only when \(a = -1\): \[ \mathbf{x}_p = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

The general solution is: \[ \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \lambda_1 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \lambda_2 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ -1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{R}. \]

2.4.2 Row-Echelon Form (REF)

A matrix is in row-echelon form if: 1. All-zero rows are at the bottom. 2. Each pivot (first nonzero entry from the left) is strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above.

This produces a “staircase” structure.

2.4.2.1 Basic and Free Variables

  • Basic variables correspond to pivot columns.
  • Free variables correspond to non-pivot columns.
    In the example: \(x_1, x_3, x_4\) are basic; \(x_2, x_5\) are free.

2.4.3 Reduced Row-Echelon Form (RREF)

A system is in reduced row-echelon form if: 1. It is in REF.
2. Each pivot is 1.
3. Each pivot is the only nonzero entry in its column.

RREF makes it easy to: - Identify basic and free variables. - Read off particular and general solutions.

2.4.4 Gaussian Elimination

Gaussian elimination systematically applies elementary transformations to bring a matrix to RREF.
This allows direct solution of \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) or \(A\mathbf{x} = 0\).

2.4.5 Example: Reduced Row-Echelon Form

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 0 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 9 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & -4 \end{bmatrix} \]

Pivot columns: 1, 3, and 4.
Non-pivot columns (2 and 5) correspond to free variables.

The general solution to \(A\mathbf{x} = 0\) is: \[ \mathbf{x} = \lambda_1 \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \lambda_2 \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ 9 \\ -4 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{R}. \]
